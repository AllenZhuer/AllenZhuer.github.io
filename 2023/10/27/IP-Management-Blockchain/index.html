This post reminds my first idea when I attended the online Fintech courses provided by Duke University on Coursera. When I initially delved into the world of blockchain technology, my mind immediately gravitated toward its potential applications in the realm of property. I’m not just talking about tangible assets like gold, cars, or real estate(RWA: real-world-asset), but also the untapped potential in Intellectual Property (IP).

Key Features of Blockchain

  1. Decentralization: This means that the database is distributed with participants in the system. If a global IP database is deployed on a high-performance blockchain, everyone can get access to a full copy. It also enables nodes that have gone offline for some time to update their database and reintegrate into the system.
  2. Consensus/ Trust: Take Proof of Work (PoW) as an example: It depends on computational power and its associated expenses: When a mathematical problem is solved through brute force, meaning the use of computational power and energy, the miner is granted the authority to append a block to the blockchain. Other participating nodes can then quickly validate its accuracy and incorporate the block into their database. The longest chain in the system serves as the definitive and authentic record in the blockchain system. And if a hacker attack happens, hackers would have to breach multiple copies of not just one central institution or central server to manipulate the entire system.
  3. Cryptography/ Secure record: In the Bitcoin blockchain and PoW, certain cryptographic technologies are essential for ensuring digital signatures and data integrity. These technologies include public/private keys and secure hash algorithms. The concept of public and private keys is employed to establish the authenticity of participants who send transactions within the system. Public keys are used to accurately identify accounts (i.e., Bitcoin accounts), while private keys are used to authenticate users or owners of Bitcoins. In the Bitcoin protocol, the private key is utilized to sign instructions for transferring Bitcoins from the owner’s account to another account. This process assures that the transaction originated from the initial Bitcoin owner.

The changing environment of IP Management:

  1. Technology mixture: Technologies and markets are converging now more than ever before. Take robots for an example, robotics demonstrate the convergence of hardware technologies, software technologies, and computer technologies, with the great potential to be applied in multiple industries. Tesla, renowned as the electric car industry leader, is venturing into the development of their robot, Optimus. Consequently, there arises a necessity for effective IP management. As a result, companies that were previously unrelated in terms of technology or business are now compelled to engage in a race to obtain patent assets, in order to sustain a competitive edge in the emerging product market.
  2. The Globalization of Business: Despite challenges like the US-China trade war, business globalization remains relentless. Firms from developed regions such as the United States, Europe, and Japan are keen on tapping into emerging markets and harnessing cost-effective labor abroad. Countries such as China, and India are undergoing swift industrial evolution, pivoting from being manufacturing hubs for other regions to innovating, producing, and globally exporting their technologically driven products. On the flip side, nations in Africa and Latin America are eager to secure employment, and investments, and elevate their citizens’ living standards. As global economies become increasingly interconnected, a disruption in one country can have far-reaching effects worldwide. And the patents will be the weapons to protect themselves.
  3. The Need for Further Harmonization of IP Law: The business world has become largely international in scope. At the same time, the policies of different countries may also be a double-edged sword. For example, the wide acceptance of generic drugs is primarily due to the fact that they cost less as compared to the original drugs. The disparities in patent law among different countries are becoming increasingly evident. When this situation is combined with the worldwide reach of the Internet, it becomes challenging to ascertain which jurisdiction holds authority over an IP matter.
  4. The Continuing Lack of a Formal IP Marketplace: As intellectual property (IP) has gained strategic importance and value over the last twenty years, the necessity for a reliable marketplace has become increasingly evident. It is apparent that the IP community has not yet accomplished this objective.
  5. A trend like China: A few decades ago, China was known as a hub of patent infringement. However, a new China is on the rise. As China’s ability to invent and innovate has grown, the Chinese have become more interested in utilizing the patent system to safeguard their own interests. And other countries are expected to follow this trend in the future to protect themselves during this transition.
  6. The rise of individual patent investment: On the buyer side, VCs used to be the primary group interested in IPs. However, with the rise of the Internet, more and more individuals are now entering this market. People are actively searching for patented technology that can bring significant wealth through licensing or selling. On the seller side, certain events occurred during the financial crisis, forcing companies to sell their patents. This was due to running out of funds or not receiving sufficient funding to complete the product. And nowadays with the combination of universities, companies, and investment, universities are happy to acquire, sell, or license their patents for great innovation.

It can be seen that a globally accessible IP platform supported by blockchain technologies has the potential to enhance the performance of IP management.

Brainstorming in Music IP rights.

Imagine Singer A desires to debut a new track. Typically, Singer A would ink a deal with management firm B, earning either a set amount or a share of profits. Then, firm B partners with streaming platforms like C, D, E, etc., garnering fees for song licenses and earnings based on play counts or a fraction of album sales. The lion’s share of profits goes to management firm B. This paradigm is what led artists like Taylor Swift to advocate for a shift, emphasizing greater remuneration for creators.

Enter Music NFTs, which promise to pivot this model in favor of artists. Firstly, the artist launches their NFT Collection A. Investors and the creator himself/herself can buy these NFTs, assisting the artist with creation, post-production, and marketing. The artist then releases their tracks, accompanied by NFT Collection B (akin to a digital album). Those purchasing Collection B obtain listening rights. However, the profits from Collection B sales primarily benefit Collection A holders.

IP Management Course @NUS

I consider myself fortunate to have attended a course on IP Management by Prof. Kenneth Huang at the NUS. This experience was transformative for several reasons:

  • Strategic Management of IP Assets: The course deepened my understanding of how IP assets have surpassed traditional physical assets in value, particularly in today’s knowledge economy.

  • Value Hierarchy and Role of IP: I learned how IP can be used to defend a firm’s position strategically, manage costs, create and capture value, synthesize opportunities, and shape the future.

  • Application of Frameworks: The course equipped me with the tools and skills to apply IP strategy frameworks and value hierarchies effectively.

This has enhanced my decision-making skills in innovation, R&D, and corporate strategies, thanks to a curriculum that included experiential learning, case studies, and real-world data.
